Problem Space

In our everyday lives, as we continue to deal with increasingly lengthy and complicated schedules, time and task management becomes difficult. We tend to lose track of our short-term goals which also affects the realization of long-term goals. This gives rise to increased stress levels and decreased productivity levels.

Design Goal: How might we improve the time management experience to help people achieve their short-term and long-term goals?

Solution

Tempo is a time management app that focuses on daily tasks for short term progress and makes repeat events or continual planning for the realization of long term goals easier.

The initial concept for this product arose from two separate ideas that were merged together to become one. The first was a vision for a habit tracking application that would help the user accomplish their long term goals, the second was a complementary idea to improve the experience of smart calendars to maximize time management and efficiency. Together, these two ideas fused to become Tempo.

User Research

Competitive Analysis

Our first step towards building the product was to analyze and evaluate the competitor space thoroughly and comprehensively. We discovered numerous competitors(both direct and indirect) that fell into a range of categories - to-do lists, habit trackers, calendars with extended functionalities, collaboration suites. Some of the top, widely-used competitors included Google Calendar and Apple Calendar. As part of the final competitive audit, we were able to identify nine different competitors and evaluated them on various metrics. We categorized our findings into strengths and weaknesses for each, thus allowing us to explore opportunities in the form of potential features for the final product and fill any existing gaps with our solution.

Some of the strengths that we identified from the competitive analysis included:

  1. Integration - being able to integrate calendars into other products across platforms.
  2. Customization - customizing your calendar to suit individual needs.
  3. Multiple Calendars - maintaining more than one calendar in the same product to satisfy different scheduling needs.
  4. Natural Language Input - scheduling according to speech recognition and a natural language understanding.

We also identified some areas of opportunity for growth which included:

  1. Maintenance - the overall effort that it takes for the user to maintain a schedule within these products.

    Design Opportunity - Reduce the overall effort to edit and maintain schedules, especially for recurring tasks.

  2. Cluttered interfaces - the more features the product contained, the more crowded was the interface.

    3. Design Opportunity - Provide a focused, clean interface to the user.

  3. Lack of motivation - none of the competitors that we looked at were particularly engaging for the users

    4. Design Opportunity - Create an immersive experience that makes interacting with calendars a fun experience.

User Interviews and Survey

After exploring the competitor space thoroughly, we moved on to conducting in-person user interviews. Over the course of one week, we interviewed a total of 12 participants. Each of us switched roles between moderators and note-takers for each individual session. Besides interviews, we also decided to conduct a survey in order to be able to generalize and validate our responses and hypotheses about time and goal management from our interviews.

Brainstorming

Affinity Diagramming

As we finished our user interviews and surveys, we decided to use an affinity diagram to synthesize our key findings and organize them into clusters based on themes. We also decided to further categorize these clusters of findings by breaking down the process of dealing with a task at hand into a "series of phases" that an individual goes through. We identified these three broad phases as Motivation (Before Action), Planning (During Action) and Reflection (After Action).

  1. Motivation (Before Action)

    Motivations to schedule or complete tasks, frequency of checking in with schedules, time management and organization styles.

  2. Planning (During Action)

    Tools and methods used to maintain schedules, what aspects they like and dislike about those, their personal preferences etc.

  3. Reflection (After Action)

    Thoughts, opinions and feelings on successfully completing or failing to complete tasks, sharing time schedules with others.

Key findings from the affinity diagram:
  • Two different kinds of motivations for making schedules: active stimulants(excitement factors) and passive stimulants(obligations).
  • Three personalities or styles of time management: free(go-with-the-flow type), aspriring(wish to manage time more effectively) and organized(keep records of all activities, big or small).
  • Two types of behavior patterns: time linear(plan ahead in time) and event occurence(make schedule when an event is going to take place).
  • People only keep long-term goals in their mind but do believe that time organization helps in their realization.
  • People derive joy from successful completion of tasks (e.g. crossing off completed tasks in a calendar or to-do list).

User Persona

We built our user persona centered around our observations from the interviews and survey.

User Journey Map

As our affinity mapping and persona helped us understand out user better and based on our results obtained from the user research thus far, we were able to surmise three important stages of the user journey:

  1. Consider (before acting on a task)
  2. Input/Execute (in the process of performing a task)
  3. Reflect (after completing a task)

Designing-Prototyping-Testing

After we conducted our initial user and stakeholder research, we used the findings from those to drive our design decisions moving forward. Before making digital screens, we first made paper prototypes and tested them with users. We felt that this would give us enough room for experimentation and provide us some initial impressions about the product idea, so we could refine and improve the design at later stages.

As we continued to brainstorm on the potential list of features for our product, we attempted to prioritize the ones that we were able to successfully tie back to our design goals informed by our user research and this allowed us to narrow down to a final list of four features that we wanted to become the Unique Selling Propositions (USPs) for Tempo.

Lo-Fi Prototypes

Once we had users interact with our paper prototypes, we quickly moved towards replicating those into digital screens. We used Figma for this. We designed low-fidelity mockups first since we wanted to capture any basic design issues while the design was still low fidelity and then use those findings and takeaways to refine our design.

User Scenarios

User Testing (Round 1)

Problem Areas Identified
  1. Users were confused between a layer and template and what each of those meant individually.
  2. Homepage seemed to be where most users would navigate to when they were confused during tasks.
  3. The process of publishing templates needed more clarity.
  4. Users expected Add (+) button to be multifunctional.
Design Decisions Taken
  1. Instead of five different navigation tabs, we cut it down to three most important ones - Home, Add (+) button and Store. Merged the tasks and homepage into a single Home.
  2. Renamed the Explore page to Store and changed its icon from globe to a cart for more clarity.
  3. Layers would now be called just calendars and templates would now just be a feature of the Store page, thus eliminating any possible confusion between the two.
  4. Pushed the calendar to the top of the homepage so it could exist as a view instead of a separate page
  5. Refined the taskflow for publishing a template on the Store page

Hi-Fi Prototypes

After conducting our first round of testing, we incorporated the suggestions and feedback from the users and began to design and prototype our medium to high fidelity prototypes. Again, we kept the five scenarios as decided, tweaking them based on newly incorporated changes. I specifically worked on the third and fourth scenarios pertaining to the design and task-flow of the Store feature. I was responsible for designing the high-fidelity mockups for each of these and also for prototyping screens within the scope of the individual scenarios, and interlinking with other screens.

User Testing (Round 2)

For our second round of testing, we tested our hi-fi prototypes remotely via UserTesting with eight participants. While some found it a little difficult to navigate around the app, most participants liked the idea behind it. They noted Store page and templates, high degree of customization, decreased manual input and automatic rearrangement of tasks as features that stood out for them and the ones they liked the most.

“This has got to be the best calendar I've ever seen because it thinks for you!”
— A participant from UserTesting.com

Reflection and Lessons Learned

Tempo was the first opportunity for me where I worked on a full-fledged product design project from start to finish and was involved in each stage of the product design process - ideation, user research, design, protoyping and testing. Lack of time is a common issue in everyone’s lives. Designing for that was important. It was a challenging project since we had to make a sincere attempt to not just differentiate it from the competition but also have it be functional and valuable at the same time to its potential users. Our team spent a considerable amount of time during the research phase which threw us a little off our planned schedule and led us to work within crunched deadlines during the later part of the project.

I learned immensely from this project and from my very capable teammates some very effective research and design practices, how to align design and product goals and most importantly, the value of collaboration. I truly believe that Tempo served as an honest attempt for us to weave established features of today's products with novel features like machine learning, layered interface, task completion, and thematic templates.
