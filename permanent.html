Permanent.org Landing Page Design Idea

Time Taken

  • 4 Hours

Project Type

  • Individual
  • One-Off Design Sprint Exercise

My Roles

  • Visual Design

Tools Used

  • Adobe Photoshop
  • Pen/Paper

Background

Based out of Austin, Design Jam is a design meetup series organized by the amazing Elijah Wood for designers to meet monthly to discuss project ideas and have fun working on design solutions to a variety of design prompts. At one such jam that I attended in October of last year Jerry Peters, Senior Software Engineer from Permanent Legacy Foundation joined us for the design prompt for the evening. Permanent.org is a nonprofit organization based in Austin, Texas dedicated to realize the unique mission of providing permanent and accessible data storage to all people for their lifetimes.

Design Prompt

How might we communicate the urgency of digital preservation through the invitations platform users send to their friends and family to join and collaborate on the platform?

Solution

A friendly, welcoming landing page design that emphasizes the pricelessness and permanence of memories and communicates the need to preserve them effectively with an equally thoughtful copy.

Design Process

As part of the evening, Elijah introduced us all to the Design Sprints method, originally created by Jake Knapp at Google Ventures in 2010. Generally conducted over a 5-day period, the sprint is a quick and easy process for answering critical business questions through design, prototyping, and testing ideas with customers. It consists of five stages - Understand, Sketch, Decide, Prototype and Validate - with each stage of the sprint to be finished in a day, from Monday through Friday. Since this was a learning activity in a meetup setting, it limited the final outcome. By the end of it, we could only get to the Design/Prototype stage and did not have a chance to test or validate our designs.

Credits: Google Ventures Design Sprint

Sketching Ideas

For the Understand phase, we were asked to think about all the different possible ideas for invitations that the users of the service would send to their families and friends to join Permanent. I quickly jotted down some keywords, design goals and rough ideas for reference. All the while, I had a constant focus on how to successfully convert a new user and convince them to join the service, while meeting the original design goals.

  1. Introduce users to a new service.
  2. Communicate the features of the service to these new users.
  3. Differentiate service from existing cloud storage solutions (Dropbox, Google Drive etc.).

For the invitation, I chose to go with a landing page design that could meet these three goals.

As I worked through the different ideas, I thought about the reasons or motivations as to why a user would like to try out this service. Would it be free extra space? Would it be credit in some form, or an added layer of security? After all this, as I moved towards sketching some rough design ideas, one thing became very clear: the design had to be centered around memories in order to convey Permanent's message effectively. But how to underscore the importance of memories in a design, and what could be the best way to capture that feeling of nostalgia through that design? The first thing that struck me was to use a cover flow for pictures with a timeline that is meant to indicate photograph history in a chronological order. Once I had the design in mind, I began to think about the potential copy. It had to be short, impactful and interesting enough at the same time to enable the user to take some action, in this case sign up for the service.

Crazy 8's

The next thing that Elijah introduced us all to was the Crazy 8's. It's a great way to quickly test different ideas (under 5 mins) before moving on to the final designs. We were asked to think about the different media channels and ways that the invite could be sent to new users. Once I had the initial ideas and sketches out of my way, I quickly sketched 8 different ideas before I finalized my landing page design idea.

Final Design

For the final design, I decided to use Adobe Photoshop since that was one design tool that I was relatively comfortable using at that time and was still learning how to use more effective tools such as Sketch and Figma. In hindsight, if I would have gained enough competency using those two, I would have probably used them instead of Adobe PS since they offer more flexibility and thus, faster workflows. Nonetheless, I was happy with the final outcome given the limited amount of time that we had in our hands.

Reflection and Lessons Learned

This session introduced me to the Design Sprints method. I liked how it is a really quick, dirty and easy way to generate ideas, narrow down and build upon them, all in a matter of hours. Like a much quicker and scrappier version of Lean UX. Sometimes scrappiness is needed in design when you don't have time to experiment a lot with your ideas and have deadlines to meet. I believe that this controlled and timed nature of the activity facilitates completion of tasks while also providing room for experimentation and enables quicker decision making, which is very critical when working in professional settings.

This was one of the first meetup experiences for me where I got to know different designers and talk to them. Elijah was a great Design Sprint facilitator for the session and Jerry from Permanent.org was very receptive of the feedback and discussed some of these ideas with some of us in person as well. I'm grateful to both for this small but valuable learning opportunity. and look forward to attending more such events in the future! :)
